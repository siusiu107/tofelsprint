Passage 0088: Serotinous Cones and Fire Regimes in Lodgepole Pine Forests (Ecology)
===================================================================================
(Word count: 615)

Wildfire is often spoken of as a disaster, yet in many ecosystems it is also a recurring
ecological force that shapes species traits. In the lodgepole pine forests of western North
America, one of the most striking fire-linked traits is serotiny: the tendency for cones to
remain sealed with resin and to open mainly in response to heat. A stand of pines can
therefore carry a “seed bank” in its canopy, released not gradually year by year but
abruptly after a crown fire.

Serotiny makes sense when fires are intense enough to kill adult trees and frequent enough
that relying on slow, ground-based seed survival would be risky. When a crown fire races
through a stand, it can kill mature pines, but it also provides the heat needed to melt
resin and open cones. Thousands of seeds can then fall onto a landscape with reduced
competition and, often, mineral soil exposure that favors germination. [A] In this scenario,
death and renewal are linked by the physics of heat and by the timing of release.

The trait is not uniform across regions. In areas where fires historically occurred at
intervals of several decades, serotiny can be common; in areas where fires are rare or low
intensity, non-serotinous cones that open without fire can dominate. The difference reflects
evolutionary trade-offs. Keeping cones sealed protects seeds from predators and weather, yet
it also risks losing them if cones decay or if trees die in a way that does not provide the
necessary heat. Moreover, serotiny can be costly in years without fire because fewer seeds
are dispersed annually. [B] A strategy that excels under one fire regime can underperform
under another.

Recent changes in climate and disturbance have complicated this match. Warmer temperatures
can dry fuels, potentially increasing fire frequency or severity in some regions. At the
same time, outbreaks of mountain pine beetle can kill large numbers of trees, altering fuel
structure. Whether beetle-killed stands burn more intensely is debated, but the interaction
illustrates a broader point: when multiple disturbances overlap, historical fire regimes may
no longer predict future ones. If fires become too frequent, young trees may burn before
they mature enough to produce cones, undermining regeneration. If fires become less frequent
due to suppression, serotinous stands may accumulate dense fuels and then experience
unusually severe fires.

Management decisions therefore matter for evolutionary futures. Fire suppression can reduce
small, frequent burns, allowing fuels to build. Prescribed burning can reintroduce fire in
controlled conditions, yet it is socially and logistically challenging. After severe fires,
managers may reseed or plant, but planting can favor certain genotypes, potentially altering
trait frequencies. [C] In effect, humans now participate in selecting which fire-adapted
strategies remain viable.

Serotiny is thus a reminder that traits are fitted to regimes, not to single events. [D]
When the rhythm of fire changes, the advantage of holding seeds in sealed cones can flip,
turning a formerly resilient strategy into one that struggles to keep pace with the new
tempo of disturbance.
